Our group published a paper in *Environmental Science & Technology* on atmospheric wet deposition.

The study developed a new approach for the accurate simulation and attribution of atmospheric nitrogen and sulfur wet deposition. Wet deposition is an important process connecting air pollution with ecosystems, but its fine-scale spatiotemporal characteristics and driving mechanisms remain uncertain.

The research team built a high-resolution machine-learning simulation system that integrates an explainable framework with additive attribution. By combining multi-source observational data with meteorological, emissions, and land-use information, the team constructed an atmospheric nitrogen and sulfur wet-deposition dataset at 0.1° × 0.1° resolution.

Using extreme-gradient boosting as the core model and introducing additive attribution methods, the study quantified the contributions of different drivers while improving interpretability. The model showed strong performance in independent testing and station-scale comparisons. It outperformed traditional chemical transport models in simulating nitrate, sulfate, and ammonium deposition, and robustly captured spatial distributions, seasonal variation, and extreme characteristics.

The results reveal long-term trends in wet deposition across different chemical species: oxidized nitrogen and sulfur deposition generally declined, while reduced nitrogen continued to increase. This pattern indicates structural differences in pollution-control effectiveness across emission sources. The study also found clear differences among land-use types, with agricultural and forest regions showing higher sensitivity to policy responses, while urban and grassland regions displayed more complex variation.

Further analysis shows that wet-deposition changes are jointly driven by emission intensity, precipitation processes, and land use. Precipitation plays a dominant role in wet-deposition formation, while ammonia emissions associated with agricultural activities are an important driver of current increases.

By combining high-accuracy prediction with mechanistic interpretation, this study provides an expandable framework for the quantitative assessment of long-term atmospheric nitrogen and sulfur wet deposition, and offers data support for coordinated emission-control strategies.
