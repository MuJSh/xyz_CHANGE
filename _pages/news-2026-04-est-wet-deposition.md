Our group developed a new method for the accurate simulation and attribution of atmospheric nitrogen and sulfur wet deposition.

Atmospheric deposition is an important process connecting air pollution with ecosystems, but its fine-scale spatiotemporal characteristics and driving mechanisms remain highly uncertain.

The research team developed a high-resolution machine-learning simulation framework that integrates explainable modeling with Shapley additive explanations. By combining multi-source observational data with meteorological, emissions, and land-use information, the team constructed an atmospheric nitrogen and sulfur wet-deposition dataset at 0.1° × 0.1° resolution.

![Machine-learning framework for atmospheric wet-deposition simulation and attribution](https://faculty.sdu.edu.cn/_resource/fileshow/dd48f7fa-e37d-48c4-968b-210b5802a044.png)

*Machine-learning framework for atmospheric wet-deposition simulation and attribution.*

The study used extreme gradient boosting as the core model and introduced Shapley additive explanations to quantitatively identify the contributions of different driving factors. This approach improves the interpretability of model outputs while maintaining prediction accuracy. The model showed strong consistency in independent testing and site-scale comparisons. Its simulations of nitrate, sulfate, and ammonium deposition substantially outperformed traditional chemical transport models, and it robustly reproduced spatial distributions, seasonal variations, and extreme-value characteristics.

Based on this framework, the team revealed long-term trends in different forms of deposition. Oxidized nitrogen and sulfur deposition generally declined, while reduced nitrogen continued to increase, indicating structural differences in the effectiveness of pollution control across emission sources. Deposition also differed significantly among land-use types: cropland and forest regions were more sensitive to policy responses, whereas urban and grassland regions showed more complex patterns of change.

Further analysis showed that deposition changes are jointly driven by emission intensity, precipitation processes, and land use. Precipitation plays a dominant role in wet-deposition formation, while ammonia emissions associated with agricultural activities are an important factor behind current increases.

By combining high-accuracy prediction with mechanistic interpretation, this study provides an expandable methodological framework for the quantitative assessment of long-term atmospheric nitrogen and sulfur deposition, and offers data support for coordinated control of different emission types.
