Recently, Professor Yuqiang Zhang and Professor Likun Xue from the Institute of Environment Research at Shandong University published a new study in *Earth System Science Data* (IF = 11.6), a leading journal in the Earth sciences. The paper is titled "Improved global daily nitrogen dioxide concentrations from 2005 to 2023 derived using a deep learning approach." The study developed a global high-resolution (0.1° × 0.1°) daily surface nitrogen dioxide (NO2) concentration dataset covering nearly 20 years, providing key data support for assessing long-term population exposure risks and environmental health effects worldwide.

NO2 is an important atmospheric pollutant that affects air quality, ecosystems, and human health. It also participates in the formation of secondary pollutants such as ozone and fine particulate matter. However, long-term, continuous, global-scale assessment of surface NO2 exposure remains challenging because of the uneven distribution of ground monitoring sites, the difficulty of directly retrieving near-surface concentrations from satellite observations, and uncertainties in chemical transport model simulations.

To address these challenges, the research team integrated multi-source big data and developed a global daily 0.1° × 0.1° surface NO2 concentration dataset for 2005–2023 based on the Air Transformer deep-learning framework. The dataset combines high spatial resolution, daily temporal continuity, and global coverage, substantially improving the accuracy of air-pollution exposure assessment.

The results show that global surface NO2 exposure has displayed significant spatial and temporal disparities over the past nearly two decades. High-exposure regions are mainly concentrated in urban clusters with dense populations, intensive industrial activity, and concentrated traffic emissions. The dataset can be further combined with population distribution and socioeconomic data to identify exposure inequalities across regions and populations, evaluate the effectiveness of clean-air policies, and provide a data foundation for air-quality management, public-health protection, and environmental-equity research.

Jiangshan Mu, a PhD graduate of Shandong University and now a postdoctoral researcher at Duke University, is the first author. Chenliang Tao, a PhD graduate of Shandong University and now a postdoctoral researcher at Fudan University, is a co-first author. Professor Yuqiang Zhang and Professor Likun Xue are co-corresponding authors. This work was supported by the National Key Research and Development Program of China.
