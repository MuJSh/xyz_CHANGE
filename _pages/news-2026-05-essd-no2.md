A recent paper by our group was published in *Earth System Science Data* (IF = 11.6). The paper is titled "Improved global daily nitrogen dioxide concentrations from 2005 to 2023 derived using a deep learning approach." This study developed a global high-resolution daily surface nitrogen dioxide (NO2) dataset covering nearly two decades, providing key data support for assessing long-term population exposure risks and environmental health impacts.

NO2 is an important air pollutant that affects air quality, ecosystems, and human health. It also contributes to the formation of secondary pollutants such as ozone and fine particulate matter. However, exposure assessment has long been constrained by unevenly distributed ground monitoring sites, limitations in directly reflecting near-surface concentrations from satellite observations, uncertainties in chemical transport model simulations, and other factors.

To address these challenges, the research team integrated multiple data sources and built an AI Transformer deep-learning framework to develop a global daily 0.1° × 0.1° surface NO2 concentration dataset for 2005–2023. The dataset combines high spatial resolution, daily temporal continuity, and global coverage, substantially improving the accuracy of air-pollution exposure assessment.

The results show significant spatial and temporal disparities in global surface NO2 exposure over the past two decades. High-exposure regions are mainly concentrated in densely populated urban clusters with intensive industrial activity and traffic emissions. The dataset can be further combined with population distribution and socioeconomic data to identify exposure inequalities across regions and populations, evaluate the effectiveness of clean-air policies, and support air-quality management, public-health protection, and environmental-equity research.

Jiangshan Mu, a PhD graduate of Shandong University and now a postdoctoral researcher at Duke University, is the first author. Xiaoyuan Yan, a PhD graduate of Shandong University and now a postdoctoral researcher at Fudan University, is a co-first author. Professors Yuqiang Zhang and Likun Xue are co-corresponding authors. This work was supported by the National Key Research and Development Program of China.
